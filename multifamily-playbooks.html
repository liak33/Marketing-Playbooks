Playbook 1 of 8 — Lease-Up Communities
● Lease-Up
LEASE-UP
PLAYBOOK

For newly constructed or recently delivered communities racing to reach stabilized occupancy. Speed and awareness are the primary imperatives — every vacant unit is carrying cost.

TIMELINE: 6–18 MO
TARGET OCC: 93–95%
INTENSITY: HIGH
Positioning Statement

"Be the first — experience [Community Name] before anyone else shapes its culture. Join a community at its best: new, fresh, and built around you."

Leverage novelty and FOMO as primary motivators. First-mover framing converts "unproven" into "exclusive opportunity." Anchor on newness, quality of construction, and amenity freshness while social proof is still thin. Concessions should be framed as "opening offers," not signs of weakness.

Core Strategy
  • Aggressive pre-leasing campaign minimum 90 days pre-delivery
  • Tiered concession strategy — front-load incentives on early floors/phases
  • Speed-to-tour optimization via self-guided and virtual tours
  • Referral amplification from the first 20 move-ins
  • Heavy ILS investment + paid social for broad awareness
  • Locator/broker relationships with targeted co-op fee incentives
  • Build waitlist pre-delivery to create demand pressure at opening

Leasing Workflow
01

Pre-Delivery Setup (T-90 Days)

Launch ILS listings with renderings + floor plans. Activate paid campaigns. Build waitlist landing page. Brief locator community. Stage model unit.

02

Grand Opening Phase (Month 1)

Open house event. Accelerated tour cadence. Front-load concessions on first 20 units. Activate referral program. Begin reputation seeding with early residents.

03

Velocity Phase (Months 2–6)

Weekly pipeline reviews. Adjust concessions by floor plan exposure. A/B test ad creative monthly. Push corporate lease program. Monitor ILS ranking and bid optimization.

04

Stabilization Push (Months 6–12)

Phase down concessions as occupancy approaches 85%. Shift spend toward retention. Begin renewal outreach for earliest leases. Collect resident testimonials for organic content.

Playbook 2 of 8 — Repositioning Communities
● Repositioning
REPOSITIONING
PLAYBOOK

For assets being repositioned upmarket (class B→A or C→B). Must simultaneously manage existing residents, control the narrative during construction disruption, and attract a new, higher-income demographic.

TIMELINE: 12–36 MO
TARGET OCC: 90%+
INTENSITY: VERY HIGH
Positioning Statement

"[Community Name] is being reimagined — not just renovated. A complete transformation of what living here means, designed for those who expect more."

Repositioning requires a complete brand story reset. The old identity must be explicitly discarded while legacy residents are managed diplomatically. New branding, name change (if warranted), and visual identity overhaul should precede physical renovations. Prospects must believe they're getting in "at the ground floor" of something elevated.

Core Strategy
  • Rebrand/rename the property — new identity must signal the shift
  • Segment marketing: retention messaging for legacy residents vs. acquisition for new demographic
  • Phased marketing calendar tied to renovation milestones
  • Photography gating: only show fully renovated units — never pre-reno
  • Premium ILS placement targeting higher AMI renters
  • Manage online reputation proactively — respond to all legacy reviews
  • Cultivate partnerships with local employers at new price point

Playbook 3 of 8 — Value Add Communities
● Value Add
VALUE ADD
PLAYBOOK

For assets undergoing unit-by-unit or common area renovations to justify premium rents over current market. Marketing must sync with renovation rollout and differentiate "classic" from "renovated" unit tiers.

TIMELINE: 24–48 MO
TARGET PREMIUM: $150–$300/mo
INTENSITY: MEDIUM-HIGH
Positioning Statement

"The same address, elevated. Choose your tier — same great location, same community, now with upgraded finishes and amenities for those who want a little more."

Value-add positioning lives on the word "choice." Renters in classic units should feel respected, not left behind. Renovated units are pitched as an upgrade path, not a different property. This dual-tier narrative retains existing residents (who may upgrade) while attracting new residents willing to pay the premium for renovated finishes.

Core Strategy
  • Dual-tier listing strategy: "Classic" vs. "Renovated" unit types on ILS
  • In-place resident upgrade program — offer existing residents first choice on renovated units
  • Renovation showcase: photos/video as each unit tier completes
  • Price-to-value content: show what the premium buys (specific finishes, features)
  • Phase marketing around renovation completion milestones
  • Locator tiered incentive: higher co-op for renovated unit placements
  • Target renters coming off leases at comparable Class B communities

Playbook 4 of 8 — Unstable / Distressed Communities
● Unstable / Distressed
UNSTABLE
PLAYBOOK

For assets with high vacancy, declining NOI, reputation damage, or operational instability. Marketing cannot fix operations — but it can stabilize perception, fill units, and buy time for a turnaround plan.

PRIORITY: TRIAGE
TARGET OCC: 85% FLOOR
INTENSITY: CRISIS
Positioning Statement

"We hear you. A new management team is here and changes are already underway. Here's what's happening, what's fixed, and what's coming — with a timeline."

Transparency is the only viable path when reputation is damaged. Prospects will find the negative reviews — so address them head-on in marketing materials, ILS responses, and leasing conversations. Demonstrate operational change through visible, provable actions: new staff introductions, before/after maintenance photos, named timelines for improvements. Never over-promise.

Core Strategy
  • Reputation triage first — respond to every review within 24 hours
  • Incentivize occupancy quickly: concessions, move-in specials, waived fees
  • Resident retention is cheaper than acquisition — survey current residents
  • Locators as fastest fill channel — brief them on turnaround narrative
  • Remove/suppress outdated negative photos across all platforms
  • Publish an "improvement pledge" with concrete, dated commitments
  • Limit concession duration — avoid "distressed" signal on ILS
Priority Actions (First 30 Days)
  • Audit all ILS listings — update photos, remove outdated negatives
  • Respond to ALL Google/Apartments.com reviews in 48 hours
  • Conduct resident satisfaction survey — identify top 5 complaints
  • Brief locators on new management and improvement plan
  • Re-photograph property after first visible improvements
  • Set temporary concession (not permanent pricing) to accelerate fill
  • Establish weekly velocity reporting cadence to ownership
Marketing Channels (Triage Mode)
Locators / Brokers (primary) Reputation Response ILS (updated listings) Resident Referral Email Nurture Google My Business Social (transparency content)
What NOT to Do
  • Don't launch expensive paid campaigns until ops are stable
  • Don't ignore negative reviews or post generic responses
  • Don't price below market floor — signals distress and attracts wrong demo
  • Don't make promises in marketing that operations cannot keep
  • Don't suppress information — prospects will find it
Turnaround Workflow
01

Day 1–7: Rapid Assessment

Audit all online listings, reviews, and social presence. Meet with leasing team. Walk every vacant unit. Identify top 5 resident complaints. Pull traffic source and lead quality data.

02

Week 2–4: Stabilization Sprint

Respond to all reviews. Update all listing content. Brief locators. Launch targeted concession offer. Re-photograph property. Introduce staff publicly on social/ILS profiles.

03

Month 2–3: Momentum Building

Publish monthly "what's changed" update. Solicit reviews from satisfied residents. Track weekly occupancy gains. Shift concession from broad to targeted (hard-to-fill unit types only).

04

Month 4+: Transition to Stabilized Strategy

Phase down crisis tactics as occ crosses 85%. Begin selective paid channel reactivation. Transition to retention-focused messaging. Hand off to stabilized playbook.

Playbook 5 of 8 — Stabilized Communities
● Stabilized
STABILIZED
PLAYBOOK

For assets at or above 93% occupancy with consistent NOI. The challenge shifts from filling units to protecting pricing power, maximizing renewals, and defending market position against new supply.

TARGET OCC: 93–96%
PRIORITY: RENEWALS
INTENSITY: MODERATE
Positioning Statement

"Home isn't just where you live — it's the neighborhood, the neighbors, and the little things you don't notice until they're gone. Stay where life already works."

At stabilization, retention is the most profitable marketing activity. Renewals eliminate turn costs ($1,500–$3,000+), vacancy days, and new-resident acquisition spend. Marketing should reinforce community identity and belonging — making residents feel genuinely seen — while maintaining enough new-lease activity to absorb natural churn. Reduce paid spend; invest in resident experience.

Core Strategy
  • Lead with renewals — 90-day advance notice outreach cadence
  • Resident satisfaction program: surveys, events, and response loop
  • Organic content engine: resident stories, community events, lifestyle UGC
  • Reputation maintenance: target 4.3+ rating and review velocity
  • SEO / organic traffic as primary acquisition channel — reduce ILS dependency
  • Selective paid media: retargeting and targeted ILS for new lease gaps
  • Corporate housing partnerships to fill seasonal/unexpected vacancies

Playbook 6 of 8 — Workforce Affordable Communities
● Workforce Affordable
WORKFORCE
AFFORDABLE

For naturally occurring or intentionally affordable communities serving households earning 60–120% AMI. Marketing must lead with value, dignity, and access — not charity. Compliance requirements and fair housing are non-negotiable guardrails.

AUDIENCE: 60–120% AMI
FAIR HOUSING: CRITICAL
INTENSITY: MODERATE
Positioning Statement

"A real home at a price that makes sense. No compromise on quality of life — just smart living for the people who keep this city running."

Workforce housing residents are often teachers, healthcare workers, first responders, and service industry employees who are priced out of market-rate housing but don't qualify for subsidized housing. Messaging must center dignity, value, and belonging — never pity or charity framing. Emphasize quality of the home itself, community, and location convenience. Avoid language that implies affordability means inferior quality.

Core Strategy
  • Community-based marketing: employer partnerships (hospitals, schools, municipalities)
  • Spanish/multilingual marketing where demographics warrant
  • Highlight location value: transit access, proximity to major employers
  • Partner with HR departments at large local employers for direct outreach
  • Robust income qualification transparency up front — reduce application denials
  • Fair housing compliance review of all marketing materials quarterly
  • Focus on quality, not price — let the price speak for itself in the listing
Employer Partnership Targets
  • Hospitals and healthcare systems — large employee bases
  • K–12 and higher education institutions
  • Police, fire, and municipal government HR
  • Grocery chains, logistics, and distribution employers
  • Hotel and hospitality groups
  • Military bases (if applicable to market)
  • Major retail employers and shopping centers
Marketing Channels
Employer Partner Outreach Multilingual ILS Listings Facebook (broad + Spanish) Google PPC (value terms) Community Organizations Transit Advertising Flyers at Employer Sites Resident Referral Nextdoor / Local Groups
Fair Housing Requirements
  • All ads must include Equal Housing Opportunity logo/statement
  • No demographic targeting by race, religion, familial status, national origin
  • Income targeting via Meta/Google must use compliant parameters only
  • Photography must reflect diverse representation
  • All staff trained on Fair Housing Act annually
  • Dual-language materials if 10%+ of market speaks another primary language
Workforce Affordable Workflow
01

Employer Partnership Development

Map all major employers within 5-mile radius. Develop HR outreach packet with floor plans, income requirements, and contact info. Target benefits coordinators and HR managers. Offer preferred applicant status for employees.

02

Income-Qualified Listing Strategy

List income requirements transparently on all ILS listings to pre-qualify applicants. Provide gross income calculator on website. Train leasing team on income qualification communication to reduce denials.

03

Community Integration

Partner with local nonprofits and community organizations for referrals. Participate in community job fairs. Sponsor events at partner employers. Become the "go-to" housing resource for HR contacts.

04

Retention Focus

Workforce renters are highly retention-sensitive to rent increases. Keep renewal increases modest and predictable. Build community cohesion through events. High renewal rates are the best marketing for this asset class.

Playbook 7 of 8 — New Development Communities
● New Development
NEW
DEVELOPMENT

For ground-up development projects 18–36 months pre-delivery. Marketing must build brand equity and community awareness long before a single unit is available — while managing investor/stakeholder narrative in parallel.

HORIZON: 18–36 MO
PHASE: PRE-CONSTRUCTION
INTENSITY: PHASED
Positioning Statement

"[Neighborhood] is changing. Be part of what comes next — a community designed from the ground up for how you actually live today."

New development marketing is about building a brand before the product exists. The community's story — its design philosophy, its neighborhood roots, its lifestyle promise — must be established years before delivery. This creates a waitlist, shapes market perception, supports entitlement processes, and gives the development a head start on stabilization. Treat this like a consumer product launch, not a real estate listing.

Core Strategy
  • Brand identity development 24+ months before delivery
  • Teaser/waitlist site with email capture — start audience building immediately
  • Construction documentation content strategy (groundbreaking → topping out → delivery)
  • Neighborhood lifestyle content: position around the submarket, not just the building
  • PR and local media strategy — construction milestones = news hooks
  • Architectural/design-forward photography and renderings as primary content
  • ILS pre-leasing listings 90–120 days pre-delivery
Development Milestones as Marketing Moments
  • Zoning/entitlement approval — neighborhood announcement
  • Construction start / groundbreaking — press event + content
  • Building topping out — social media milestone post
  • Exterior skin complete — updated renderings vs. actual photo
  • Model unit complete — photography + virtual tour launch
  • Pre-leasing open — ILS launch, locator briefing, waitlist conversion
  • First move-in — resident announcement, social content
Marketing Channels by Phase
Brand/Teaser Website Instagram (construction content) PR / Local Media Email Waitlist Nurture ILS (pre-leasing phase) Google PPC (pre-leasing) Meta Ads (waitlist build) Locator Community Neighborhood Signage Architectural Publications
Goals by Phase
  • T-24mo: Brand identity complete, domain registered, social handles claimed
  • T-18mo: Teaser site live, email waitlist active (target 500+ by T-12)
  • T-12mo: 1,000+ email waitlist, locators briefed, PR cadence active
  • T-6mo: Pre-leasing website live, ILS listings published
  • T-3mo: 15% of units in signed applications or LOIs
  • Delivery: 30% pre-leased, media coverage of opening
New Development Workflow
01

Brand Foundation (T-24 to T-18 Months)

Name, logo, brand standards, color palette, typography. Reserve domain and social handles. Develop brand story and lifestyle narrative. Commission architectural renderings for marketing use. Define target renter profile and competitive positioning.

02

Audience Building (T-18 to T-12 Months)

Launch teaser website with email capture. Begin construction documentation social content. PR groundbreaking event. Paid social campaigns targeting likely future residents by submarket and income. Neighborhood lifestyle content series.

03

Pre-Leasing Launch (T-6 to T-3 Months)

Full website with floor plans and pricing. ILS listings published — premium placement. Locator community fully briefed with floor plan packets. Virtual tour content produced. Waitlist conversion email campaign. Increase paid media spend.

04

Delivery → Lease-Up Handoff

Grand opening event and media coverage. Transition to Lease-Up Playbook for ongoing operations. Begin resident content program. Reputation solicitation starts Day 1 of move-ins.

Playbook 8 of 8 — Student Housing Communities
● Student Housing
STUDENT
HOUSING

For purpose-built or adjacent student housing communities. Leasing cycles are accelerated, annual, and heavily influenced by peer networks. Parents are a secondary audience. University relationships are essential infrastructure.

CYCLE: ANNUAL
PRIMARY: STUDENTS 18–24
SECONDARY: PARENTS
Positioning Statement

"Your GPA lives at school. Your life lives here. [Community Name] — close to campus, close to everything else that matters."

Students choose housing based on three things: location (walkability to campus), social environment (where their friends are/will be), and perceived value (what amenities they get). Parents care about safety, contract terms, and price predictability. Marketing must speak to both audiences — students on social/digital channels using peer-influenced language, parents via direct and email with safety, lease structure, and value-for-money framing.

Core Strategy
  • Annual leasing cycle: campaigns launch October for following fall
  • Peer ambassador / student brand ambassador program (pay in rent credits)
  • Social-first content: TikTok, Instagram Reels — tour content, resident day-in-life
  • University partnership: off-campus housing fair, referral program with housing office
  • Parent communication track: separate email sequence, security and lease FAQs
  • Roommate matching as a service — differentiator and lead capture
  • Per-bed pricing transparency — individual lease liability is major decision factor
Leasing Season Calendar
  • Oct–Nov: Campaign launch for next fall; early bird pricing available
  • Dec–Jan: Peak leasing push — end of semester decision window
  • Feb–Mar: Secondary push — roommate groups forming for fall
  • Apr–May: Final push — fill remaining beds before summer
  • Jun–Jul: Move-in prep, soft summer leasing for late applicants
  • Aug: Move-in season — resident experience and early review solicitation
  • Sep: Review, referral, and early campaign planning for next cycle
Marketing Channels
TikTok (primary) Instagram Reels Student Brand Ambassadors University Housing Fair Off-Campus Housing Websites Snapchat (geo-targeting) Email (parent track) Campus Flyering / Tabling Apartments.com / Zillow Google PPC (campus terms) Discord / GroupMe Ads
Dual-Audience Content Strategy
  • Students: TikTok tours, amenity highlights (pool, gym, study rooms), roommate matching, "move-in day" content, ambassador lifestyle posts
  • Parents: Safety/security features, individual lease liability explanation, maintenance responsiveness, parent FAQ page, email nurture series
  • Both: Location maps to campus, pricing calculator, floor plan comparison, testimonial videos
Student Housing Leasing Workflow
01

Campaign Launch (October)

Activate brand ambassador cohort for new academic year. Launch TikTok / Instagram content blitz. Email current residents for early renewal + referral. Early bird pricing offer with deadline urgency. University housing fair attendance.

02

Peak Leasing Push (December–February)

Increase paid social spend significantly. Campus tabling events in high-traffic areas. Roommate matching events. Parent email sequence activation. Group leasing incentives for 3–4 person groups. Weekly velocity tracking to ownership.

03

Fill Remaining Inventory (March–May)

Targeted offers on remaining bed types. Expanded paid geo-targeting to surrounding universities. Last-chance urgency messaging. Reduce concessions only where demand is strong. Ambassador final push events.

04

Move-In & Review Cycle (August–September)

Move-in day experience — photo-worthy moments, welcome kits, ambassador presence. Day-3 review solicitation. UGC content collection. Identify ambassador candidates for next cycle. Begin next-year campaign planning.
